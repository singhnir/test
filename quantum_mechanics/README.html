Quantum Mechanics

Suppose we have solved a time-indepedent Schrodinger equation for some potential *(unperturb system: solution known)*: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \hat {H}^{(0)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle = E_n^{(0)} |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle \end{aligned} \end{equation} and we are interested in solving for the slightly different potential system *(perturb system: solution unknown)*: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \hat {H}|\psi_n\rangle = E_n |\psi_n\rangle \end{aligned} \end{equation} To begin with $\hat {H}$ can be split into the sum of two time-indepedent parts: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \hat {H}&= \hat {H}^{(0)} +\hat {H}_P \\[1ex] \hat {H}_P&= \lambda \hat {H}^1 \end{aligned} \end{equation} The main idea is that the perturbed eigenvalues and eigenstates can both be expanded in power series: two time-indepedent parts: \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} &|\psi_n\rangle= |\psi_n^{(0)}\rangle + \lambda|\psi_n^{(1)}\rangle + \lambda^2|\psi_n^{(2)}\rangle + \cdots \\[1ex] &E= E_n^{(0)} + E_n^{(1)} + E_n^{(2)} + \cdots \end{aligned} \end{equation}
